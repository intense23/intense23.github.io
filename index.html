InteNSE'23

A one-day workshop to discuss the opportunities and challenges in neural software engineeing and analysis.

What is Neural Software Engineering?

Due to recent computational advancements in Deep Learning (DL), data-driven tools and algorithms have become an inseparable part of different research areas in the Software Engineering. DL has shown to improve and revolutionize many SE tasks such as code manipulation, program repair, program synthesis, software testing, debugging, and symbolic analysis. We consider the neural software engineering (NSE) as a broad research area at the intersection of machine learning (ML) and software engineering (SE).

Why is it important to develop robust and interpretable neural software engineering?

Currently, most research in software engineering treat ML models as closed-box and opaque. However, this treatment brings many challenges to the interpretability and robustness of NSE.
  • DL models are notorious for adversarial example attacks that can mislead neural models to make an unintended prediction. In the NSE setting, such vulnerability can result in subtle, and often unnoticed bugs in software. Without ensuring the adversarial robustness, there is a risk that neural models perform poorly on real codebase.
  • Closed-box models can only be evaluated through generic metrics. As a result, one cannot truly determine and validate if the superiority of a new model is due to better embedding of code, fine-tuning, the architecture, or data leakage. Without such understanding, the community only blindly progresses on designing better neural models for software analysis.
  • Since the ultimate end user of NSE is software developers, a neural model that automates specific tasks without giving convincing high-level explanations to developers about the whys and the hows in the automation process, would be unsatisfactory and often useless in practice.


What is the goal of InteNSE workshop?

While SE community needs to enforce Interpretability and Robustness as the primary evaluation metics, two crucial prerequisites still need to be met: (1) Awareness and (2) Knowledge. At InteNSE, we aim to advance the state of ML4SE research by considering four main components keynotes, research papers, hackathon, and a panel. The first two components of the workshop aim to contribute to the knowledge about different interpretability techniques and robust developments of NSE. The latter two components aim to foster awareness for the SE community about the pitfalls of ignoring interpretability and robustness of NSE. Co-located with ICSE'23, InteNSE'23 will include 1) two keynotes, 2) research paper presentations, 3) hands-on Hackathon, and 4) the panel of expert discussions.

Call For Papers

InteNSE is an interdisciplinary workshop that targets research at the intersection of programming languages and software engineering (PLSE) and ML. We welcome submission of research papers that consider interpretability and robustness as first-class citizens in the (1) design of neural models for code and the (2) evaluation of the resulting ML4SE techniques.

Topics of interest include, but are not limited to the following software engineering and analysis tasks:

  • Robustness of NSE
  • Interpretability of NSE
  • Source Code Analysis
  • Program Synthesis
  • Program Repair
  • Software Testing
  • Software Debugging
  • Neurosymbolic Programming
  • Prompting

The authors are highly encouraged to discuss the interpretability and robustness of their proposed approach in their submissions.

Submission Format

Submissions must conform to the IEEE conference proceedings template, specified in the IEEE Conference Proceedings Formatting Guidelines (title in 24pt font and full text in 10pt type, LaTeX users must use \documentclass[10pt,conference]{IEEEtran} without including the compsoc or compsocconf options). The official publication date of the workshop proceedings is the date the proceedings are made available. This date may be up to two weeks prior to the first day of ICSE 2023. InteNSE will employ a double-blind review process. No submission may reveal its authors' identities. The authors must make every effort to honor the double-blind review process. In particular, the authors' names must be omitted from the submission, and references to their prior work should be in the third person. These papers should be at most 6 pages in length.

The paper submission deadline is 13 January 2023 AoE (UTC-12h).

Hackathon

this component aims to involve researchers in interpreting and measuring the robustness of existing ML4SE pre-trained models. Precisely, participants will (1) assess how and why the closed-box neural models for software engineering tasks to make specific decisions and (2) evaluate the robustness of such models against adversarial inputs. We believe such a hands-on activity can significantly enhance the awareness and knowledge of the SE community about neural software analysis.

Expert Panel

The expert panel will comprise people from academia and industry, with a research background in neural software analysis. The organizers will prepare a list of questions, including questions from the participants in the workshop, to lead the discussions. The primary goal of this panel is to identify the extent to which interpretability can enhance the usage of automated neural software analysis for practitioners. Furthermore, the panel will serve as a platform for panelists and other participants to discuss open challenges and a promising roadmap in advancing the role of interpretability and robustness in ML4SE research.
